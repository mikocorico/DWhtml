Since our topic had to be related to a digital phenomenon that is present in our daily lives, we started at, well, the beginning and thought about what digital platform we encounter every single day of our lives. That was obviously the Web . From then on, we tried to find some variable that acts somewhat as the lowest common denominator across the Web today that plays an important role in our lives at the same time (besides the programming language). It is not new that we are easily influenced by what we see on the Web, but what is it exactly? Is it the content of the page or the layout and its appearance that is speaking more loudly to our brains? Or both? This lead us to ask the following critical issue that has become the central theme of our project:

To what extent does the appearance and design of websites influence how we perceive the authenticity of its content?

Now, you can find anything and everything on the Web, and since we could not possibly cover every aspect of the appearance of websites which would have led to too much of a generalisation, we chose the online clothing retailing sector , focusing solely on websites from brands and retails that also have a physical retail store besides their online presence. The reason why we chose this specific topic was because we found it to be in one of the more personal spheres of our private life . We believe that choosing what clothes you want to wear, what design they have, what brand it is etc. is a very personal choice , meaning that the individual is more likely to be alert of the authenticity of the website , setting it as a prerequisite (in contras t to when you simply want to look up a definition of a word for example). In addition to that, data shows and forecasts that ecommerce clothing will very likely continue to rise in the next years, making it a most relevant topic for us today.

Of course, we are aware of the fact that, as with everything in this world, there are a lot of other/outside factors that play a role in the choice of the buyer to ultimately decide to get a piece of clothing. Therefore, we had a look at what experts had to say about what tendencies people have and what they take into account when shopping for clothes in general.

The Methods

In order to keep in step with the spirit of this project, we decided to go for online surveys to collect the necessary data for our analysis. We also found it to be the most relevant and effective method, since we do live in the Internet Age. Furthermore, we assumed that people were much more likely to fill out an anonymous online survey an d give us more accurate and true answers than if we were to ask random individuals on the street. The variety and richness to the results will also be evident since we would not be geographically limited; meaning we could easily send the survey to our friends of all ages and backgrounds all across the world with an easy click (or two). We are aware of the downside of this as well however, namely that variables of the data sample will be fixed depending on who decides to take this online survey rather than us deciding whether we want to ask a young woman of Asian origin rather than an old man of French origin (for example).

Target audience/profiling

The target audience of our survey are college students from 17-22. Our profiling question regarding age is set so that any response falling outside of this range could be set aside of our main consideration.

We have chosen this particular age group of individuals for two main reasons. Firstly, young college students are often assumed to be tech-savvy and fashion-sensitive at the same time. So they might be more responsive to differences in the designs of online retailing websites. Moreover, this target group is chosen for their accessibility as well. Since the survey is conducted through an online social network within a college setting, a larger pool of survey responses can be collected by targeting the dominant group in the given context.

Choice of websites

We decided to ask respondents to choose which website seemed the most authentic/trustworthy to them.
In our survey, respondents had the choice between 2 similar websites for each of the 8 sets of questions.
Set 1 to 4: For each set, we chose 2 similar websites (homepage) in terms of brand recognition, products (male or female clothes, similar price…) and overall design. Our aim was to limit bias of the respondents by presenting 2 similar websites, even if we are aware that they might have preferences for some brands, or might be influenced by personal preferences for design.
Then, from set 5 to 8, we focused on one particular product per set (keeping the same websites from set 1 to 4).

Preference questions

There are a few factors that we have highlighted at the end of each set of questions in asking about the reason for which they have chosen one option or the other. It is for the purpose of narrowing the focus of our research that we have provided these few factors that we suggest the respondents to look at. However, we have also provided an option for indicating any other considerations that have affected the respondents’ choices, so as to paint a fuller picture.

iFrames

iFrames, instead of screenshots or links, are chosen as the medium for displaying the website designs in our survey, for their interactivity and dynamism.

We considered screenshots as a more direct medium, compared to links, that encourage intuistic choices. However, the static nature of screenshots is not fully-satisfactory in rendering the more dynamic aspect of websites. Since not only images, but also many other interactive features are considered in designing website displays. As a result, we resorted to using iFrames, which allow users to have the full experience of browsing a website while completing the survey without having to exit the survey page.

Computer vs. mobile display

Depending on the width of the iFrame, the embedded website shifted to either website or to mobile display. Knowing that the user experience is different for both (computer display allows you to move more around in the website for example), we wanted to uniformize all the displays into the mobile phone display. However, it was not possible for set 4 and 8 websites (they stayed in computer mode). Thus, we asked at the end of the survey if the respondents noticed a difference between sets 4 and 8 and the other sets, to take into consideration the potential influence that a type of display could have on their choice.

The Results

We would like to thank all those who did get complete the survey, as well as all those who supported us along the production of this statistical inquiry.
The Aesthetics project is part of Sciences Po's "Introduction to Digital Culture" assessment.

As predicted and expected, the appearances of the websites influence our decisions a lot, which naturally has an effect on our lives – even if it is only to a small extent for some people. In the light of the analysis of our data, we can say that it is much easier/harder (depending on the point of view) to convince people in this digital age, where the authenticity of the data one can gather online is not always equivalent to the neatness of its layout. It’s easier for people or organisations of non-serious or less formal nature to convince people by simply putting a bit more effort (or to invest more) into the design of their webpage. There are software that give you access to a pre-designed, neat and authentic-looking website that you can construct the way you want, skipping the (tedious) step of coding the whole page oneself. On the contrary, it might be harder for authentic personas, organisations or institutions to persuade the public of their authenticity, since they are “on par” with all the other organisations of various levels or seriousness/legitimacy behind the blue-light screen. A good example are official government websites, that often lack in aesthetic pleasantry.
